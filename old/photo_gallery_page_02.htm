"A Conversation With the Author,
Writer/Photographer/homeless Veteran,
James D. Sewell, Part II."
As told to Sgt. Chuck Myerson, "Voices" Editor.

"Of all the things I've done in my life, producing this photo-documentary was undoubtedly the most difficult. It took 5 years to complete and it required that I live on the streets of NYC for most of that time. It demanded that I be there, to record the truth about "the phenomenon of homeless Veteran", up front and personal.

I have lived with, taked to and photographed hundreds of homeless Veterans in all 5 boroughs of NYC. I have lived with them and worked with them in all kinds of situations and under all kinds of circumstances.

I have recorded "the phenomenon of homeless Veteran" on the most beautiful sunny day and in the cruelest weather imaginable, from mind numbing cold to stifling summer heat, in blinding snow and monsoon rains.

I have lived under loading docks and bridges, slept on sidewalks and in doorways, and lived in abandoned cars. I have been to places, seen things and heard things that no one but another homeless Veteran would believe or understand. I have seen the tears, heard the cries and felt the pain of those who believed they had been abandoned by America.

I have fought with the best of them, drank with the worst of them, prayed with some and rolled in the gutter with others. There were times when I feasted like a king and other times when I was so hungry and broke I ate from dumpsters.

And of all the reasons I have heard for being homeless, none, including my own, were as poignant as the words of an old, homeless Veteran, who had been living on the streets of NYC for the last 8 years, who said, "We are homeless because America has forgotten us."

I have done all of this in search of the truth about "the phenomenon of homeless Veteran."

What I found was "a national tragedy in full bloom", from a frail 79 year old WW II Army Veteran, desperately clinging to his last shred of dignity, to a young female Veteran who had been raped in Basic Training, and never been treated for the trauma, to a Desert Storm combat Veteran, who had been refused treatment for his Gulf War Syndrome, all living on the streets of America,along with hundreds of thousands of other homeless Veterans.

And even in spite of how difficult the homeless experience was, it was all made even worse by public apathy, and in some cases, public hostility.

I have seen homeless Veterans, both men and women, subjected to all kinds of harrassment and ridicule, from the local police as well as from total strangers, the very people that they so honorably served in the military.

But in spite of all the indignities many were forced to endure, pride in their military service and love for America was still in the hearts of most of the homeless Veterans that I talked to.

To see that, to experience first hand, the strength that it takes to "keep the faith", was well worth the price of admission.

Seven out of ten homeless Veterans said, unequivocally, that they would gladly do it all again, "for God and Country." Even the broken and weary said they would "gladly put their lives on the line again, for America."

When I questioned those who had been subjected to the worst treatment, the answers they gave were almost always the same, "God Bless America, land of the free, home of the brave."

As usual, it was the broken and the weary that were the most vulnerable. And, as usual, it was those they trusted that betrayed them. Those who were entrusted to help homeless Veterans were the very ones who stole from us most often. The injustices committed against America's homeless Veterans by social service organizations was painful to see and even more painful to be subjected to.

What little the social service organizations did provide for homeless Veterans was almost always of the poorest quality. It was not unusual to hear comments like, "Yesterday I was eating garbage from dumpsters. Today I'm eating garbage from a paper plate", or hear questions like, "Why do they treat us so disrespectfully?"

The only answer is, we were nothing more to them than profit, "a number in a people warehouse."

I was surprised and angered at how often homeless Veterans were victimized and taken advantage of by those who were entrusted to provide services. I was just as surprised to learn that very seldom were any of the perpetrators prosecuted or otherwise disciplined. In other words, they all got away clean, with homeless Veterans left holding the bag, still trying to pick up the pieces of a shattered life.

When I first began to record this phenomenon, I did so with the hope and expectation that a real solution could be found. As a homeless Veteran with professional writing and photo-journalistic skills, I beleived that it was incumbent upon me, more than anyone else I knew, to produce an intelligent document, for the purpose of finding a viable solution.

The real value of this document is "as a lesson in how not to approach the problem of "homelessness among America's Armed Forces Veterans." Common sense and decency demand that a better alternative be provided for America's homeless Veterans.

The best and most reliable approach always has been, "... give a man what he needs to help himself", because self-help will always provide the best answers.

Our solution is based entirely on self-help. Our corporate motto/philosophy directly addresses several of the key elements of the problem; "Self-help is the only real answer. You re-build it...you own it, is the only real solution."

This" real solution" involves the reclaiming and renovation of abandoned, tax delinquent residential properties, by homeless Veterans.

It will work because distressed real estate can be found in every major city in American. It will work because many homeless Veterans already have the necessary construction trade skills taht are needed to make it work. It will work because those skills can easily and quickly be learned by anyone. It will work because, "It is self-help and personal achievement that restore hope and self respect."
